❁ Pedagogy

Assessing experiential learning

With the FUTURE in Biology team, I am involved in a number of collaborative projects assessing the short- and long-term impacts of experiential learning on student outcomes and persistence in the sciences. These assessments utilize a combination of institutional datasets, quantitative student outcomes data, and qualitative survey and interview data to explore wide-ranging factors, including course design, cognitive and affective impacts of experiential learning, departmental/institutional pathways and predictors of experiential learning participation, community-building and social network formation within these experiences, and more.

One ongoing assessment I am leading explores how prior participation in experiential learning (field-based or research-based courses and programs) impacts student performance, knowledge retention, and metacognitive skills transfer in subseqent required lecture courses. [paper in prep]

Inclusive experiential course design

Our team also has several ongoing projects focused on inclusive course design in experiential and field-based teaching contexts. In a cross-institutional collaborative paper that offers a set of field teaching principles, we argue that inclusive field course design has the potential to foster students' connection to people and culture, place and the natural world, their own relationship to learning, and their identities. [paper in revision]

In development: An ongoing collaboration develops an experiential course design framework that defines four experiential design axes (place & context, immersion, inquiry, relevance) and for pedagogical support axes (teaching & mentorship, inclusion, iteration, community & collaboration) with examples of teaching strategies to guide educators who are new to inclusive, experiential course design, and to provide a framework for education researchers to more precisely describe the design properties of courses in educational assessments.

Equity in online course outcomes

Along with collaborators at UCSC's Teaching & Learning Center, I've investigated the effect of participation in intentionally designed online pre-requisite courses on student outcomes in post-requisite courses. Our investigation reveals that online pre-requisite completion does not negatively impact post-requisite grades; furthermore, online pre-requisite completion does not exacerbate equity gaps in downstream outcomes. Taken together, our results suggest that intentionally designed online courses are equally as effective at contributing to equitable educational outcomes for marginalized students. (with Megan McNamara, Herbie Lee, and Michael Tassio) [submitted for publication]


❁ Psycholinguistics

Higher-order linguistic units in memory

Through my dissertation work, and in joint work with several collaborators, I experimentally investiagted the relationship between higher-order (i.e., prosodic or discourse) structures and memory for syntactic content. Extending work on how prosodic chunks and discourse segments relate to units in memory, my work focuses on (i) what types of cognitive operations apply at "significant" linguistic boundaries, and (ii) how memory retrieval operates across these domains.

Four lines of ongoing, related work investigate the following:

  • Memory and metacognition in clause wrap-up effects (with Hannah Hausman and Matt Wagers)
  • The effect of prosodic boundaries on syntactic reanalysis (with Matt Wagers)
  • Contiguity effects and encoding interefence between linguistic domains (with Stephanie Rich & Matt Wagers) [poster]
  • Memory retrieval at the prosody-discourse interface in appositive relative clause constructions (with Jack Duff, Pranav Anand, & Amanda Rysling) [poster]

Focus-prosody interactions in memory

Through a series of auditory recognition memory studies, I'm exploring how memory for focus structure and memory for prosodic detail interact at a delay, following a large body of work on the deeper encoding of foci compared to non-foci in memory. (with Morwenna Hoeks, Nick Van Handel, Cal Boye-Lynn, and Amanda Rysling) [poster]


❁ Formal semantics & pragmatics

Ellipsis & the structure of discourse

I've investigated a type of pragmatics-sensitive ellipsis termed argument-reversing verb phrase ellipsis (e.g. A: I1 love you2. B: I2 do, <love you1>, too.), where pairs of participants intuitively reverse roles across the antecedent and ellipsis clauses. I argue that these mismatches of argument structure are possible only when the context allows for accommodation of a reciprocal Question Under Discussion structure (Do A and B love each other?). [SuB handout & talk] [paper]

Aligning outlooks in discourse

I've worked on other topics in semantics and pragmatics, including attitude ascriptions in contexts with subjective and objective Questions Under Discussion (QUDs). Using naturally-occurring data from a corpus of dyadic conversations, we argue that conversational partners pursue the strategy of contributing non-comittal stances in order to align outlooks, even in response to objective QUDs. (with Jack Duff) [paper]
